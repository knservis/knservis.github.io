# FAQ

## 1. What are the differences between a psychiatrist, a psychologist and a psychoanalyst?

Psychiatrists and psychologists both understand brain function, thought processes, and behaviour, and use counselling and psychotherapy to help with mental health issues. Psychiatrists can prescribe medication, while psychologists cannot.

Psychologists help with anxiety, depression, and mental health assessments. For severe conditions requiring medical intervention, psychiatrists may be more appropriate. Psychoanalysts offer long-term therapy based on psychoanalytical theory.

---

## 2. What are the main differences between psychoanalytic and psychodynamic psychotherapy?

While both therapies aim to explore the unconscious mind and its influence on behaviour, psychoanalytic therapy is more intensive and long-term, whereas psychodynamic therapy is shorter, more flexible, and often more collaborative.

### Psychoanalytic therapy

- **Origin:** Developed by Sigmund Freud
- **Focus:** Explores unconscious mind, repressed desires, fears, unresolved conflicts
- **Techniques:** Dream interpretation, free association, resistance and transference analysis
- **Duration:** Long-term, several years, multiple sessions weekly
- **Setting:** Patient reclines on couch, therapist out of view

### Psychodynamic therapy

- **Origin:** Based on psychoanalytic principles but more flexible
- **Focus:** Current emotions and relationship patterns integrated with past insights
- **Techniques:** Eclectic, combining psychoanalytic, cognitive, and behavioural methods
- **Duration:** Shorter-term, typically weekly sessions
- **Setting:** Face-to-face, therapist actively engaged

---

## 3. Is psychodynamic therapy suitable for me?

Psychodynamic therapy benefits those seeking deeper self-understanding, working through impactful past experiences, achieving fulfilling lives, addressing persistent issues unresponsive to other treatments, and exploring connections between various mental health challenges.

---

## 4. Is psychoanalysis therapy suitable for you?

Psychoanalysis therapy suits individuals who seek to:

- Explore deep-seated thoughts and feelings
- Understand behavioural root causes
- Commit to long-term therapy
- Address persistent mental health issues
- Gain profound self-awareness
- Discuss dreams and feelings in non-directive settings

---

## 5. First consultation with a psychologist: what to expect?

- **Introduction and rapport building:** The psychologist introduces themselves, explains their background, approach, and therapy expectations.
- **Information gathering:** Questions about reasons for therapy, current concerns, goals, personal history, family background, past experiences.
- **Discussion of therapy process:** Explanation of the therapy type practised, benefits, confidentiality, session frequency, and logistics.
- **Setting goals:** Therapist and client outline therapy objectives together.
- **Questions and clarifications:** Opportunity to ask about the process, approach, or any concerns.
- **Initial impressions:** A chance to assess the psychologist's style and your comfort level.

---

## 6. How to choose your therapist?

The foundation of long-term consultations is to offer a safe, non-judgemental space for patients to express themselves authentically. Trust (confidentiality, consistency) and quality exchanges (feeling understood) are crucial for progress.

Therapists like Carl Rogers and Irving Yalom emphasise empathy, congruence, and unconditional positive regard. Selecting a therapist where you feel safe and understood is vital.

---

## 7. What are transcultural psychiatry and transcultural group therapy?

**Transcultural psychiatry** is a field that examines how cultural and social factors influence mental health and illness. It aims to understand the impact of cultural differences on the expression, diagnosis, and treatment of psychiatric disorders.

**Transcultural group therapy** involves groups of therapists working together to address the specific needs of patients from diverse cultural backgrounds, focusing on cultural, social, and historical contexts. Sessions typically include interpreters alongside psychiatrists, psychologists, and anthropologists.

## 8. What is a psychological evaluation and what tests do therapists use?

Psychological evaluations diagnose mental health conditions, understand personality traits, and assess cognitive abilities. Standardised tests are one component alongside clinical interviews, observational data, and medical history.

- **MMPI-2:** Assesses mental health disorders and personality structure
- **WAIS:** Measures cognitive ability and adult intelligence
- **Stanford-Binet:** Evaluates intelligence and cognitive abilities across age groups
- **Beck Depression Inventory (BDI):** Assesses depression symptom severity
- **Rorschach Inkblot Test:** Analyses personality characteristics and emotional functioning
- **TAT:** Explores underlying motives and social perception

---

## 9. Who could benefit from group therapy?

Group therapy involves one or more therapists working with small groups simultaneously, offering several benefits:

1. **Support and shared experiences:** A sense of community that can be incredibly validating and comforting.
2. **Diverse perspectives:** Members benefit from others' varied perspectives and coping strategies.
3. **Skill development:** Communication, social interaction, and problem-solving practised in a safe environment.
4. **Cost-effective:** More affordable than individual therapy.
5. **Accountability and motivation:** Members encourage commitment to therapeutic goals.

Group therapy suits individuals facing mental health conditions, life transitions, or social skills challenges. Groups of 5–15 members typically meet weekly for 1–2 hours.

## 10. Key concepts differentiating Freud, Jung, and Lacan

### Sigmund Freud

- **Unconscious mind:** Pioneer of psychoanalysis, introducing the unconscious housing repressed desires, memories, and emotions.
- **Psychosexual development:** Personality develops through stages (oral, anal, phallic, latency, genital).
- **Oedipus complex:** Child's desire for opposite-sex parent and rivalry with same-sex parent.
- **Defence mechanisms:** Ego mechanisms (repression, denial, projection) manage id and superego conflicts.

### Carl Jung

- **Collective unconscious:** Shared reservoir of experiences and archetypes common to all humans.
- **Archetypes:** Universal symbolic images (Hero, Shadow, Anima/Animus) influencing behaviour.
- **Individuation:** Process of integrating different self-aspects to achieve wholeness.
- **Synchronicity:** Meaningful coincidence of causally unrelated events.

### Jacques Lacan

- **Return to Freud:** Reinterpreting Freud, emphasising language and the symbolic order.
- **Mirror stage:** Developmental phase where infants identify with their reflections, forming the ego.
- **The Real, the Imaginary, and the Symbolic:** Three registers structuring human experience.
- **Desire and the Other:** Desire mediated by the Other (symbolic order, language).
